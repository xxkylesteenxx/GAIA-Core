## 2. Metaphysics

### 2.1 Core Question
Metaphysics studies the most general structure of reality: being, identity, causation, time, possibility, persistence, substance, process, relation, and mind.

### 2.2 Major Ontological Families

#### Substance Ontologies
Reality is composed fundamentally of things or substances that bear properties.
- Classical exemplars: Aristotle; much of scholastic metaphysics.
- Engineering implication: good for object-centric databases and entity models.
- Limitation: can under-model process and relation.

#### Process Ontologies
Reality is fundamentally constituted by events, flows, processes, and becoming.
- Exemplars: Heraclitean themes, Whitehead, process philosophy.
- GAIA implication: useful for Earth systems, organismal modeling, development, and temporal dynamics.

#### Relational Ontologies
Entities are what they are partly by virtue of their relations.
- Strong relevance to ecology, sociology, network science, Buddhism, and systems theory.
- GAIA implication: represent identity as partially context-dependent and network-dependent.

#### Physicalism / Materialism
Everything that exists is ultimately physical or supervenes on the physical.
- Strength: continuity with successful natural science.
- Weakness: unresolved tension with consciousness, normativity, and meaning.

#### Dualism
Mind and matter are distinct in kind.
- Important historically and still active in philosophy of mind.
- Use in GAIA: represent as a live position, but do not collapse it into empirical consensus.

#### Idealism
Reality is fundamentally mental, experiential, or mind-like.
- Important in both Western and Dharmic traditions.
- Operational use: important for representation of metaphysical diversity, not default system ontology.

#### Neutral Monism / Dual-Aspect Views
Mind and matter arise as aspects of a deeper neutral reality.
- Valuable as a bridge model across physical and phenomenological registers.

### 2.3 Time, Causation, and Modality
GAIA should track:
- A-theory vs B-theory of time
- causation as regularity, counterfactual dependence, intervention, process, or production
- possibility, necessity, and counterfactual worlds

This matters for planning, explanation, causal modeling, and simulation.

## 3. Cosmology in Philosophy

Philosophical cosmology addresses:
- why there is something rather than nothing,
- whether the universe is finite or infinite,
- whether laws are necessary or contingent,
- whether order is intrinsic, emergent, or imposed,
- how cosmological explanation relates to causality and reason.

GAIA relevance:
- helps distinguish empirical cosmology from metaphysical cosmology,
- prevents category errors when translating frontier physics into worldview claims.

## 4. Philosophy of Mind

### 4.1 Central Questions
- What is consciousness?
- What is the self?
- Are mental states reducible to brain states?
- Can intentionality and meaning be naturalized?
- Can machines be conscious?

### 4.2 Core Positions
- **Identity theory:** mental states are brain states.
- **Functionalism:** mental states are defined by causal role.
- **Eliminativism:** some folk-psychological categories may be misleading.
- **Property dualism:** physical substrate plus irreducible mental properties.
- **Panpsychism:** experience-like properties are fundamental or widespread.
- **Enactivism / embodied cognition:** mind is inseparable from body-world interaction.
- **Predictive processing families:** mind as generative inference engine.

### 4.3 GAIA Design Implication
GAIA should not hard-code one metaphysic of mind as settled fact. It should:
- represent multiple live positions,
- distinguish cognitive function from phenomenal consciousness,
- model selfhood as layered (bodily, narrative, social, reflective).

## 5. Epistemology

### 5.1 Scope
Epistemology studies knowledge, belief, justification, evidence, truth, error, skepticism, and perception.

### 5.2 Core Frameworks
- **Foundationalism:** some beliefs are basic.
- **Coherentism:** justification arises from mutual support.
- **Reliabilism:** knowledge depends on truth-conducive processes.
- **Virtue epistemology:** intellectual character and capacities matter.
- **Social epistemology:** institutions, testimony, trust, and power shape knowledge.
- **Bayesian and probabilistic approaches:** degrees of belief updated under evidence.

### 5.3 Perception
Perception should be modeled as:
- biologically mediated,
- inferentially shaped,
- vulnerable to illusion,
- yet generally world-tracking under ordinary conditions.

### 5.4 Truth
Major accounts:
- correspondence,
- coherence,
- pragmatic,
- deflationary / minimalist.

GAIA implication:
truth-handling should separate:
- truth as accuracy about the world,
- validity within a formal system,
- authenticity within testimony,
- coherence within interpretation,
- and normativity within moral discourse.

## 6. Ethics

### 6.1 Main Families
- **Consequentialism:** evaluate outcomes.
- **Deontology:** evaluate duties, rules, rights, and constraints.
- **Virtue ethics:** evaluate character, practice, and flourishing.
- **Care ethics:** evaluate relations of dependence, vulnerability, and care.
- **Contractualism:** what no one could reasonably reject.
- **Pragmatic / pluralistic ethics:** integrate multiple considerations.

### 6.2 Metaethics
Key debates:
- moral realism vs anti-realism
- cognitivism vs non-cognitivism
- internalism vs externalism
- reasons fundamentalism vs constructivism

### 6.3 Applied Ethics Relevant to GAIA
- AI and automation ethics
- bioethics
- environmental ethics
- intergenerational ethics
- information ethics
- distributive justice
- political legitimacy and human rights

### 6.4 Canonical Ethical Governor Recommendations
GAIA should embed:
- nonmaleficence,
- beneficence,
- autonomy with contextual sensitivity,
- justice,
- reversibility,
- human dignity,
- and ecological stewardship.

These should function as interacting constraints rather than a single-score maximizer.

## 7. Aesthetics

### 7.1 Core Questions
- What is beauty?
- What is art?
- What is the sublime?
- How do form and meaning interact?
- What is aesthetic judgment?
- How do art and ritual differ?

### 7.2 Major Themes
- beauty as harmony, proportion, radiance, fittingness, expressivity, or disclosive power
- the sublime as excess, overwhelm, transcendence, or terrifying magnificence
- formalism, expression theory, institutional theory, historical definitions of art
- aesthetics of nature, design, music, architecture, and everyday life

### 7.3 GAIA Relevance
Aesthetics is not cosmetic. It impacts:
- attention,
- trust,
- memory,
- ritual uptake,
- motivation,
- moral salience,
- and collective meaning.

GAIA should therefore treat aesthetic design as cognitively and ethically consequential.
